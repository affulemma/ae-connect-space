PREFACE
I wish someone had warned me early. You may ask, “Warned
you about what?” Not about exams. Not about lecturers. But
about the silence after the excitement fades, the pressure that
comes from nowhere, and the freedom that can either build
you or break you. When I first stepped into university, I
quickly realized that no one truly prepares you for what
happens beyond matriculation. When you are entering tertiary,
everyone hypes you: ―Go and make us proud.‖ ―Just learn
hard. ―You are now on your own.‖ But nobody tells you what
happens when you finally get there. Nobody tells you how
quickly one bad decision can change your whole direction.
Nobody tells you how fast a semester can disappear if you
don‘t pay attention. Nobody admits how lonely campus can
feel even when you‘re surrounded by thousands of people. I
saw people lose themselves. I saw people find themselves.
Then I started asking myself: what makes the difference? That
question became the heartbeat of this book, FROM HIGH
SCHOOL TO FREEDOM.
From High School To Freedom was born out of observation,
personal experience, and countless conversations with students
who were struggling to balance freedom, responsibility, and
focus. I realized that university is not a joke, it is a place where
character, habits, and choices speak louder than intelligence.
Some students enter campus with confidence and leave with
regret. Others enter quietly and leave as leaders, grown, sharp,
and focused. What makes the difference is not the school. It is
not the program. It is you, yes you, your discipline, your

CHAPTER TWO
RESPONSIBILITY IS NOW PERSONAL
There comes a point in life where you must stop saying, ―They
didn‘t remind me,‖ or ―No one told me what to do.‖ That point
is now. Tertiary doesn‘t chase you. Nobody knocks your door
to tell you to go to class or beg you to study for exams. That
freedom we talked about earlier? It comes with a price and that
price is personal responsibility. Some of us came here with
habits from senior high depending on housemasters, rules,
bells, sirens, and punishments. But those systems are no longer
here. And if you don‘t let go of that mindset, university will
humble you fast. Let go of thinking someone else will always
cover for you. Let go of blaming your past or your parents. Let
go of the idea that you have all the time in the world. Let go of
every weight even friends that slow you down. This is now
your life, your future, and your responsibility. Every decision
you make here, to attend class or skip, to study or scroll, to
grow or play is yours alone. And guess what? So are the
consequences. Nobody is coming to rescue you. But you can
rescue yourself, if you are ready to let go of what is holding
you back.

CHAPTER THREE
TIME MOVES FASTER THAN YOU THINK
Here comes another crucial part of tertiary life that
students don‘t usually notice. The moment we arrive on
campus, our minds jump straight to ―When are we
vacating?‖ or ―This semester will be long,‖ so we relax,
thinking we still have time. But the truth is very different.
On campus, time does not walk-it runs. Before you realize
it, Week one has turned into Week five, the Semester
Doesn‘t Wait for You. You will blink and it‘s mid-
semester. You will scroll small on social media and boom
exams timetable is out. You will say, ―I will start next
week,‖ and before you know it, the lecturer is saying ―You
have a quiz next class.‖ Time moves crazy fast in the
tertiary and if you don‘t catch it, it will leave you behind
with no apology. Time moves fast in university, faster than
most students expect. Unlike senior high school, no one is
tracking your progress. The days feel long, but the
semester is short. If you are not intentional, it will end
before you realize you‘ve done nothing meaningful. Many
students waste the first few weeks thinking they have time.
They skip lectures, ignore assignments, and forget to
revise. Then suddenly, everything starts happening at once,
quizzes, mid-semester exams, group assignment, deadlines,
and final exams. Panic mode sets in. Here is the truth: time
management is not about having time. It’s about
making time. The best students don‘t have more time than
anyone else they just use it better. They plan ahead. They
set reminders. They create routines that protect their focus.

CHAPTER FOUR
LEARN HOW TO LEARN
Because university is not about cramming, it‘s about
understanding yourself. That is why you must learn how to
learn. And that is exactly what this chapter is here to teach you.
Let‘s begin this chapter with a truth many people learn too
late: The smartest students in university are not always the
ones who studied the hardest but the ones who studied the
smartest. You can be in the library every day and still fail if
you are using the wrong method. You can copy all your notes
and still forget everything during the test. Why? Because no
one teaches you how to learn. In high school, learning was
mostly about memorizing what the teacher said and pouring it
out in the exam. It worked because you had structure,
supervision, and regular revision. But university is a different
world. The content is heavier, the pace is faster, and you are
now your own teacher. Here is the good news: You don‘t
need to be a genius. You just need to learn how to learn based
on how your brain works best.
WHAT DOES “LEARNING HOW TO LEARN”
MEAN?
Some students fail not because they are lazy, but because they
never learned how to learn. They copy notes, re-read books,
and stay up late yet nothing sticks. Why? Because their method
doesn‘t match how the brain works. Here is how best you can
learn how to learn to become better.

KNOW YOUR LEARNING STYLE-VARK
MODEL
Everyone has their own unique ways that they learn. These
learning styles are critical for finding success in the classroom,
students who understand different learning styles are able to
focus their education and use different strategies to find the
greatest success in the classroom. Is good to know your
learning style as far as tertiary is concern. We all learn
differently. Even without noticing, some of us learn best by
―seeing,‖ others by ―hearing,‖ while other people learn best by
―doing.‖ Understanding your learning style will help you a lot
on campus. Here are the four learning styles of the VARK
model of learning, which consists of visual, auditory, reading
and writing, and kinesthetic learning.
1. ARE YOU A VISUAL LEARNER? Visual learning
style is a learning method where learners need to see,
I.e., watch, and observe to learn. The learning may
involve spatial awareness, videos, photographic
memory, diagrams, graphs, etc. Visual learners will
quickly remember places, objects, and faces because
they can quickly recollect details by creating a mental
image.
2. ARE YOU AN AUDITORY LEARNER? Auditory
learning means that a student learns most effectively by
listening. They would prefer listening to a lecture over
reading a textbook, or hearing the instructions for a
project instead of figuring it out hands-on. You
remember more when you listen to lectures or repeat
things aloud?
From High School To Freedom Page 14

===== PAGE 27 =====
3. ARE YOU A READING/WRITING LEARNER?
My people are you there, this is exactly where I belong
to. You like to take detailed notes and read them back
like me? Reading and writing is a type of learning
during which people prefer to read and write in order to
learn more effectively. These learners are known as
read and write learners and usually learn best by
reading and writing the information down. They are the
people who always add notes to pictures, choose a
hardcover book over an audiobook, and prefer using
closed captions while watching videos. Their learning
process consists of a combination between reading and
writing, by reading to understand and note-taking to
learn.
4. ARE YOU A KINESTHETIC LEARNER?
Kinesthetic learning, also known as tactile learning, is a
learning style in which individuals learn best through
physical activities such as touching, moving, or
performing hands-on tasks. Kinesthetic learners are
often described as hands-on learners who require active
participation and physical engagement to understand
and retain new information fully. You grasp things
better when doing something physical (e.g., drawing
maps, building models, moving around)? Knowing this
can change your whole study game.
NB: It‘s better to know who you are, because once you do, you
are already halfway there.

EXAMPLE
A visual learner will want to see an example of the project,
while someone who has an auditory focus will prefer to
hear about the project. A kinesthetic learner wants to try it
out for themselves, different from the visual learner and the
auditory focused learner. There's not a right or wrong
learning style, visual isn't better than the kinesthetic
learning style or vice-versa. What's important is to
understand learning strategies and study tips that can help
you in a classroom environment.
THE SMART-LEARNING SYSTEM
On campus if you want to succeed then you need to space your
days, don‘t be part of the ―LMLA-Last Minute Learners
Association‖. Don‘t cram the night before. Space your study

CHAPTER TEN
HOW TO CALCULATE GPA AND CGPA
GPA
GPA stands for Grade Point Average. It is the average of
your grades for a single semester. Each course you take has a
grade (like A, B, C, etc.) and a number of credit hours (like 2,
3, or 4). Your GPA is calculated by multiplying the grade point
of each course by its credit hours, adding them up, and then
dividing by the total number of credit hours. To calculate your
GPA (Grade Point Average), follow these steps:
STEP 1: Know the Grading Scale (Example)-UCC
Grade Score Range Grade Point
A 80 - 100 4.0
B+ 75 - 79 3.5
B 70 - 74 3.0
C+ 65 - 69 2.5
C 60 - 64 2.0
D 50 - 59 1.0
E 0 - 49 0.0
STEP 2: Multiply the grade point by the credit hours of each
course. Thus
Course Grade Point × Credit Hours = Weighted Points
STEP 3: Add all the weighted points

CGPA
CGPA is the average of your GPA across multiple semesters.
It helps you track your overall academic performance from
Level 100 to Final Year. This is very important because your
final class (First Class, Second Class, etc.) is based on your
CGPA not just your GPA from one semester.

HOW TO CALCULATE YOUR CGPA
You will need:
The GPA for each semester
The Total Credit Hours for each semester
CGPA Formula:
CGPA=Total of (GPA × Semester Credit Hours) \ Total Credit
Hours across All Semesters
