PREFACE
There are moments in life that change everything without asking for permission. One day, you
are full of excitement, making plans for the future, celebrating your achievements, and
believing that the years of sacrifice have finally begun to pay off. Then, almost overnight, life
interrupts the journey. A parent loses a job. A loved one passes away. A family business
collapses. An illness consumes the savings that were meant for your education. Suddenly, the
future you spent years preparing for seems to disappear before your eyes. It is in moments like
these that many young people quietly begin to believe that their story has come to an end.
Over the years, I have met students whose dreams were interrupted, not because they lacked
intelligence, discipline, or ambition, but because circumstances beyond their control stood in
their way. I have listened to stories filled with disappointment, silence, and unanswered
questions. I have watched brilliant young people remain at home while their classmates
continued to university, carrying a burden that very few people truly understood. Every
conversation reminded me of one important truth: sometimes life is unfair. Before we can talk
about hope, we must first be honest enough to acknowledge that reality.
This book is also for the young man or woman whose education was interrupted by teenage
pregnancy. While teenage pregnancy is not something we encourage, it is a reality that many
young people face. Too often, society responds with judgment instead of guidance, leaving
many to believe that one mistake has permanently defined their future. I want you to know that
while every decision carries consequences, those consequences do not have to become life
sentences. Your dream of education, personal growth, and a meaningful future is still worth
pursuing. Your journey may now require greater courage, responsibility, and determination,
but it has not come to an end.
This book is for the young man who had to put his education on hold because he became the
breadwinner of his family. It is for the student who lost a parent just when everything seemed
to be falling into place. It is for the young person who was forced to leave school because there
was simply no money left. It is for anyone who has ever stood at the edge of a broken dream
and wondered, "What happens now?" Whether your education was interrupted by financial
hardship, illness, family responsibilities, the loss of a loved one, teenage pregnancy, or any
other unexpected challenge, I want you to know that you are not alone. This book was written
with you in mind.
This book was born from those stories. It was not written to offer empty motivation or to
convince you that everything happens for a reason. It was written because I believe that every

young person deserves someone who understands what they are going through before telling
them what they should do next. I wanted to write a book that would sit beside the reader during
one of the most difficult seasons of life, offering understanding before advice, compassion
before instruction, and practical direction before unrealistic promises. Sometimes people do
not need another motivational speech. Sometimes they simply need someone to say, "I
understand your pain, and I want to help you find your way again."
As I reflected on the experiences of so many students, one picture continued to appear in my
mind. I imagined a young person walking along a road for many years, taking one step after
another toward a destination called success. Every examination passed was another step. Every
sacrifice made by parents and guardians was another step. Every late night of studying was
another step. Then, without warning, the road ended at a wall. Most people would naturally
believe that this was the end of the journey. Some would sit down in disappointment. Others
would turn around and give up completely. But if they looked carefully, they would discover
a smaller road hidden beside the wall-a road that was easy to overlook but still led to the very
same destination. That picture became more than an illustration. It became the foundation of
this book.
I have come to believe that many dreams are not destroyed; they are simply delayed until we
discover another route. We are living in a generation where learning is no longer limited to the
four walls of a classroom. Knowledge is no longer reserved for those who can immediately
afford university. Today, opportunities exist beyond what previous generations could have
imagined. Online education, digital skills, scholarships, student loans, entrepreneurship,
artificial intelligence, mentorship, and global communities have created new pathways for
those who are willing to keep moving forward. The tragedy is not that these opportunities do
not exist. The tragedy is that many young people never discover them because no one points
them in the right direction.
That realization is one of the reasons I founded A.E CONNECT. My vision has always been
larger than helping students gain admission into a university. I want to help young people
become lifelong learners, critical thinkers, problem solvers, and leaders who are prepared for
the changing world. Through education, mentorship, career guidance, digital skills, research,
entrepreneurship, and access to opportunities, I believe we can help many young people
discover that hidden road which society rarely talks about. This book is one part of that vision.
It is not merely a collection of encouraging words; it is the beginning of a practical roadmap
for rebuilding dreams.

INTRODUCTION
Before you read another page, I need you to know something. This is not one of those books
that tells you to simply believe in yourself or repeats the familiar phrase that "everything
happens for a reason." If that is what you are looking for, you may not find it here. I am not
writing from a place where life has always gone according to plan, nor am I writing to pretend
that every setback is easy to overcome. I am writing because I have seen too many dreams
come to a sudden stop, not because the people behind those dreams lacked intelligence,
discipline, or ambition, but because life placed obstacles in front of them that they never
expected and never deserved.
I have watched brilliant young people complete Senior High School with excitement written
all over their faces, believing that the next chapter of their lives was finally about to begin.
They worked hard, stayed up late studying, sacrificed their comfort, and carried the hopes of
their families on their shoulders. Then, almost overnight, everything changed. A parent died.
A family business collapsed. A mother or father lost a job. An illness consumed the savings
that had been set aside for university. The admission letter arrived, but there was no money to
answer it. For many students, that moment felt like watching years of sacrifice disappear in a
single day. This book is also for the young woman whose education was interrupted by teenage
pregnancy. While teenage pregnancy is not something we encourage, it remains one of the
realities that quietly steals the dreams of many young people every year. Too often, society
responds with judgment instead of guidance, making young mothers believe that one chapter
of their lives has already written the ending of their entire story. It has not. The journey ahead
may be more difficult. It may require greater responsibility, resilience, and courage than you
ever imagined. But difficulty is not the same as impossibility. One decision, one circumstance,
or one season of your life does not have the authority to determine the rest of your future.
Perhaps your story is different. Maybe you became the breadwinner of your family before you
were ready. Maybe your parents simply could not afford to continue paying your school fees.
Maybe your dream was interrupted by sickness, family responsibilities, or circumstances you
never saw coming. Whatever brought you here, I want you to know something before we
continue. I see you. More importantly, you are not alone. There are thousands of young people
standing exactly where you are today, wondering whether the dream they carried for so many
years has finally come to an end.

If that is you, I am not here to tell you that it does not hurt, because it does. I am not here to
rush you through your pain, because pain that is ignored does not disappear. It simply hides
until another day. Before this book teaches you anything about opportunities, scholarships,
online learning, entrepreneurship, or building your future, it wants to do something much
simpler. It wants to sit beside you. It wants to acknowledge your disappointment before it
speaks about your possibilities. Sometimes the first step toward healing is not finding an

I did not write this book from a distance. I wrote it as someone who believes deeply in the
power of education and who has watched countless young people in Ghana and beyond chase
the same dream of university, a degree, and a better future for themselves and their families. I
have seen how quickly that dream can begin to fade when circumstances suddenly change.
Those experiences forced me to ask myself a question that eventually became the foundation
of everything I do: What can I actually do to help? Not who is to blame. Not why life is unfair.

the end of the road? That question eventually gave birth to A.E CONNECT, and this book is
one of the most personal answers I have ever written.
As I reflected on the stories of so many students, one picture continued to stay in my mind. I

stood a destination they had dreamed about for years. Then, without warning, the road ended.

No Support. No Admission. Life Happened. Most people would naturally stop there because

route.
That picture became more than an illustration. It became the foundation of this entire book.
Every chapter you are about to read reveals another part of that hidden path. Not as a
consolation prize for people whose dreams did not work out, but as a genuine, practical, and
meaningful way forward. Because the truth is that education has changed. The world has
changed. Opportunities have changed. There are roads today that previous generations never

THE DAY EVERYTHING CHANGED
"Sometimes life doesn't announce that it's about to change forever. It simply lets you wake up

THE BOY WHO HAD ONLY ONE PLAN
Robert was the kind of student every teacher remembered by name, not because he was the
loudest in class, but because he was the most consistent. From the moment he entered Senior
High School, he carried one plan in his heart, and it never changed. He wanted to study
Engineering at a public university, graduate, secure a good job, and build a proper house for
his mother in the village where he was born. He had watched her wake up before five every
morning to sell kenkey by the roadside, rain or shine, so that his school fees could be paid on
time. He had watched his father take on extra driving jobs on weekends, ferrying passengers
between towns long after his regular work had ended, simply to make sure there was always
something left over for books, uniforms, and the small requirements schools never seem to stop
asking for.
Every sacrifice his parents made became fuel for Robert. He studied at night by lantern light
during the periods when the electricity in his community was unreliable. He formed a small
study group with three of his classmates, and together they would meet under the mango tree
behind the dormitory to test each other on past questions until the compound bell rang for
evening prep. His mates often teased him for refusing invitations to weekend outings, but
Robert had made peace with missing certain moments if it meant protecting the one thing he
was chasing. He was not trying to be perfect. He simply understood, even as a teenager, that
his family's sacrifices deserved a return, and he intended to give them one.
There was a particular evening, during his final year, that Robert still remembers clearly. His
study group had gathered under the mango tree as usual, working through a set of Core
Mathematics past questions, when one of his friends, Ishmael, looked up and asked a question

===== PAGE 17 =====
none of them had really discussed out loud before. He asked what they would actually do with
their lives once they finished all of this. One friend said he wanted to become a pilot. Another
said she wanted to study medicine and return to work in their own community, because she had
grown up watching her grandmother travel two hours just to see a doctor. When it was Robert's
turn, he did not hesitate. He said he wanted to become an engineer, build roads and bridges that
made life easier for people like his parents, and one day construct a proper house for his mother
with his own money, so that she would never have to wake up at five in the morning again
unless she chose to. Nobody laughed at him. They simply nodded, because in that small circle
under the mango tree, dreaming out loud was allowed.
When the WASSCE results were finally released, the whole house erupted. His mother, who
rarely allowed herself public displays of emotion, wept openly in the small courtyard outside
their home as neighbors gathered to congratulate her. His father, a man of few words, simply
placed a hand on Robert's shoulder and said nothing for a long moment, which Robert
understood to be the loudest expression of pride his father knew how to give. Robert had passed
with the kind of grades that opened doors.
THE SACRIFICES BEHIND THE DREAM
It is easy, when telling a story like Robert's, to skip quickly past the years of sacrifice and move
straight to the exciting part, the admission letter, the celebration, the promise of a new chapter.
But those years deserve more attention than that, because they are the very foundation the wall
would later crash into. Robert's mother sold kenkey every single morning at the same junction,
rain or shine, and there were seasons when business was so slow that she returned home with
almost nothing to show for an entire day standing in the sun. Yet she never once suggested that
Robert's school fees should wait. She would simply find a way, borrowing a small amount from
a neighbor here, adjusting the household budget there, absorbing the shortfall quietly so that
her son would never have to feel the weight of it directly.
There was one particular term, during Robert's second year, when the fees were not ready by
the deadline the school had set. Robert remembers standing in the line outside the bursar's
office with other students whose names were being called out publicly for owing fees, a practice
that felt, to a sixteen-year-old, like a quiet form of public humiliation. When his name was
called, he walked forward, collected the notice, and folded it carefully into his pocket rather
than his bag, as though hiding it from his own eyes might somehow soften the shame. That

===== PAGE 18 =====
evening, he did not tell his mother exactly what had happened at school. He simply mentioned
that fees were due soon. Three days later, without being asked twice, she had found the money.
He never learned exactly how.
His father's contribution came in a different form. He drove a taxi that had been in the family
for years, a vehicle that required constant, careful maintenance to keep running on the rough
stretches of road between their town and the nearby cities. On weekends, when most men his
age were resting after a long week of driving, Robert's father would take on additional trips,
ferrying market women and their goods between towns before dawn, returning home exhausted
but never complaining, at least not where Robert could hear him. Robert understood, even
without being told directly, that every cedi his father earned on those extra weekend trips was
quietly being set aside for one purpose. His education. This is why the admission letter, when
it finally arrived, did not feel like a personal achievement alone. It felt like a family
achievement, the visible proof that years of a mother's early mornings and a father's exhausting
weekends had not been wasted. Understanding this is important before we go any further,
because it explains why what happened next did not simply feel like a disappointment to
Robert. It felt like watching his parents' years of sacrifice collapse in front of him, and that is
an entirely different, heavier kind of pain.
THE LETTER THAT CHANGED EVERYTHING
Six months after the WASSCE results were released, the admission letter finally arrived,
delivered to their home by a neighbor's son who worked at the local post office and had
recognized the university's official stamp on the envelope. Robert was not home when it
arrived. He returned from an errand to find his mother standing at the door, holding the
envelope with both hands as though it might disappear if she loosened her grip, tears already
forming before either of them had said a word.
They opened it together, seated on the low bench outside their home, his mother's hand resting
on his shoulder as he unfolded the letter. Engineering. A public university. His name printed
clearly across the top of an official document that felt, in that moment, like proof that
everything his family had endured finally meant something. His mother read the letter twice,
then a third time, running her fingers slowly over his printed name, as though she needed her
hands to confirm what her eyes were already seeing. That evening, neighbors began arriving

one after another, drawn by the news the way news like that always seems to travel in a small
community faster than any phone call could carry it.
For the first time in years, the house felt lighter. His mother began speaking about the future
differently, no longer in the cautious tone of someone hoping for a miracle, but in the confident
tone of someone watching a miracle already unfold. She spoke about which pots and provisions
she would help him pack for the hostel. His father, still a man of few words, began quietly
asking around about the cost of a new travel bag, the kind sturdy enough to survive years of
use. Relatives called from as far as Kumasi and Takoradi to congratulate the family. Robert
began imagining his first day on campus, the hostel he might be assigned to, the friends he
might make, the man he was about to become. He allowed himself, just for a season, to believe
that the hardest part was finally behind him.
Looking back, Robert often wonders whether he should have guarded that season of joy more
carefully, whether some quiet instinct should have warned him against believing too
completely that the road ahead was now guaranteed. But he does not blame himself for that
hope, and neither should you blame yourself for any hope you allowed yourself to feel before
your own wall appeared. Hope is not a mistake. It is simply what happens when years of
sacrifice finally seem to be paying off, and no one should be asked to apologize for believing,
even briefly, that the hardest part of their story was over.
WHEN THE WALL APPEARED
Three weeks before Robert was due to report to campus, his father's taxi was involved in an
accident on the Accra to Cape Coast highway. Nobody was seriously injured, but the vehicle,
which was not fully insured, was damaged beyond what the family could afford to repair. It
was not merely a car. It was the family's main source of income, the very engine that had been
quietly funding Robert's dream for years. Overnight, the small buffer of savings his parents had
been building toward his admission fees disappeared into hospital bills, towing charges, and
the beginning of a legal matter with the other driver involved.
Robert remembers the exact moment he understood what had happened. His father returned
home two days after the accident, sat quietly at the edge of the veranda, and did not speak for
almost an hour. His mother moved around the kitchen with the kind of forced busyness people
adopt when they do not know what else to do with their hands. Nobody explained anything to
Robert directly. He simply began noticing the silences. The conversations that stopped the

moment he entered a room. The admission letter, once displayed proudly on top of the family
cupboard, quietly disappearing into a drawer.
THE CONVERSATION THAT BROKE HIM
Robert finally asked his mother directly, one evening, why nobody was speaking to him about
campus anymore, why the travel bag his father had been asking about had never been bought.
His mother sat him down on the same low bench where they had opened the admission letter
together, and this time, her hands trembled as she spoke. She told him, as gently as she could,
that there was no longer enough money to pay his admission fees, and that they did not yet
know how they would find it.
She told him that she and his father had not slept properly since the accident, that they kept
trying to find a way, but every door they knocked on either had no money or wanted something
they could not provide, and that she was sorry, deeply and truly sorry. Robert remembers sitting
completely still, unable to find any words, watching his mother, a woman who had never once
complained about selling kenkey in the rain, apologize to him for something that was never her
fault. His father stood a short distance away, arms folded, unable to look directly at his son,
and Robert understood, without being told, that his father's silence was its own kind of apology.
That night, Robert lay awake replaying the conversation over and over, searching for some
detail he might have misunderstood, some version of events where the admission letter still
meant what it had meant three weeks earlier. He found none. In an instant, the road he had been
walking since primary school, the road paved by years of early mornings, missed weekends,
and quiet sacrifice, appeared to end in front of a wall he never saw coming.
WHAT ROBERT'S STORY TAUGHT ME
I have sat across from young people whose stories echo Robert's so closely that it no longer
surprises me. A father's shop destroyed by fire weeks before his daughter's admission fees were
due. A mother's illness swallowing the family's entire savings the same month her son received
his university placement. A business collapse. Teenage pregnancy. A sudden job loss. A family
emergency that has nothing to do with a student's ability, character, or effort, yet still manages
to stand directly between that student and the future they worked years to build. Robert's story
is not unusual.

What has stayed with me most, through every conversation of this kind I have had, is not the
details of what went wrong, because the details are always different. It is the particular
expression that appears on a young person's face the moment they realize their dream has not
been cancelled by their own failure, but by circumstances entirely outside their control.
Sometimes life is simply unfair, and the only honest thing to do is admit that clearly before
trying to offer anything resembling hope.
This is exactly why A.E CONNECT exists, and it is exactly why this book exists. Not because
I have a magic solution that erases hardship, but because I have watched enough young people
stand in front of walls like Robert's to know that what they need first is not advice. What they
need first is to be seen. Everything else, the practical guidance, the roadmap, the hidden path,
comes later. For now, we simply needed to stand together in front of the wall.
I remember, distinctly, the moment I decided that watching from a distance was no longer
enough for me. It was not one dramatic encounter but an accumulation of quieter ones,
conversations with young people whose stories I recognized instantly because I had heard
versions of them before, each one carrying the same undertone of a dream interrupted by
something entirely outside their control. Somewhere along the way, I stopped simply feeling
sympathy for these stories and started asking what I could actually build in response to them.
A.E CONNECT, and this book alongside it, are the answer I eventually arrived at.
THE WEIGHT OF THAT MORNING
There is a particular kind of pain that comes from watching a dream stop, not because you

WHY IT FEELS LIKE THE END
"It is possible to do everything right and still feel like your whole world has ended. That

THE QUESTION THAT WOULD NOT LEAVE HIM ALONE
In the weeks following the conversation with his mother, Robert found himself returning, again
and again, to a single question. Why did this feel like the end of everything, when technically,
nothing about him had actually ended. He was alive. He was healthy. His results still existed
on paper, and his admission letter, though now folded quietly in a drawer, was still real. And
yet, some mornings, he woke up with a heaviness in his chest that felt indistinguishable from
grief, the same kind of grief he had witnessed at his grandfather's funeral two years earlier. He
could not understand why losing an opportunity felt, in his body, so much like losing a person.
This confusion is worth naming clearly, because many young people in Robert's position
quietly assume something is wrong with them for feeling as devastated as they do. They
compare their reaction to the situation itself and conclude that they are overreacting, that other
people have survived far worse, that they should simply be grateful they are alive and healthy.
This comparison, while well intentioned, rarely helps. It only adds a second layer of pain on
top of the first, the pain of feeling like your own pain is somehow illegitimate.
I want to spend this entire chapter answering Robert's question honestly, because it deserves a
real answer, not a dismissal. Why does this particular kind of disappointment feel so much
heavier than other setbacks life brings. The answer, as you will see, has very little to do with
weakness, and everything to do with how deeply identity, sacrifice, and time become tangled
together inside a dream like Robert's.
WHEN A DREAM BECOMES YOUR IDENTITY

For twelve years, Robert had not simply been working toward a goal. He had slowly, without
realizing it, built his entire sense of who he was around that goal. When people asked him about
himself, he did not describe his personality or his interests first. He described his plan. I am
going to study Engineering. I am going to build roads and bridges. I am going to take care of
my mother. Somewhere along the way, the goal and the person pursuing it had quietly merged
into a single identity, so that losing access to the goal did not simply feel like losing an
opportunity. It felt like losing a piece of himself.
This is one of the most important ideas in this entire book, so I want to state it as plainly as
possible. When a dream becomes deeply tied to your sense of identity, and then that dream is
interrupted by circumstances outside your control, the pain you feel is not really about the
university placement, the scholarship, or the specific opportunity that was lost. It is about a
threat to your sense of who you are. That is why the grief can feel so disproportionate to people
watching from the outside, and so completely proportionate to the person actually living
through it.
Psychologists who study identity often describe this phenomenon using the idea of a possible
self, the version of a person that exists only in imagination, built from years of hope and
planning. Robert's possible self was an engineer, walking across a graduation stage, handing
his mother the keys to a house he had built for her. That possible self had lived vividly in his
mind for years, rehearsed so many times that it had started to feel almost as real as his actual,
present life. When the wall appeared, it was not only Robert's admission that was threatened.
It was the sudden, jarring loss of that imagined future self, and grieving the loss of an imagined
self is, in many ways, just as real as grieving the loss of anything else you have loved.
It helps to understand that this possible self was never actually destroyed, only paused, though
it rarely feels that way in the early weeks of a crisis like Robert's. The version of Robert who
becomes an engineer, who builds his mother a house, who stands on a graduation stage, is still
available to him, still reachable, simply by a route he had not yet imagined needing to take.
This book will spend real time later helping you rebuild a clear, workable image of your own
possible self, adjusted for your actual circumstances rather than abandoned because of them.
For now, it is enough to notice that the grief you feel is grief for something real, even if that
something was, in the end, still only a possibility rather than a guarantee.

quietly carrying despite their smiling photographs. Robert was comparing his very real, very

THE NIGHT ROBERT FINALLY CRIED
It happened almost two months after the conversation on the low bench with his mother, on an

What surprised Robert most, in the days that followed that night, was not the crying itself, but

every grieving person eventually discovers if they allow themselves to. Grief that is finally

WHY WE TEACH CHILDREN NOT TO CRY
Robert had grown up, like most boys in his community, absorbing a quiet but persistent
message about tears. Big boys do not cry. Men handle their problems in silence. Crying is
something reserved for small children and for women, and even then, only in private, only

him this. It had simply seeped into him through a thousand small moments, a father's raised

is often especially pronounced in how many African communities raise their sons. Girls are

make others uncomfortable, like grief, sadness, and fear.

space to grieve it honestly, they are handed, often with good intentions, a series of instructions
for how to appear unaffected. Be strong for your mother. Do not let people see you struggling.
Put on a brave face. These instructions rarely come from cruelty. They usually come from
adults who are themselves uncomfortable with visible grief, often because nobody ever gave
them permission to grieve honestly either. The pattern repeats itself, generation after
generation, until someone finally decides to interrupt it.

taught about crying, about strength, about what a capable, resilient young person is supposed

it becomes unbearable, but simply because crying is one of the most natural and healthy

THE CYBER CAFE ON MARKET STREET

nationally by the Commission for Technical and Vocational Education and Training, offered
structured pathways into engineering related trades that did not require the same upfront fees

something called Recognition of Prior Learning, a process that allows people who have gained
practical skills informally, through work, through apprenticeship, through self study, to have
those skills formally assessed and credited toward a recognized qualification. He discovered
free online courses on platforms like Alison and Coursera, some offering completion
certificates in technical drawing, basic engineering principles, and project management,

A WORLD THAT NO LONGER EXISTS
To understand why this discovery mattered so much, it helps to understand the world Robert's
parents grew up in, a world that, in educational terms, barely still exists today. When Robert's
father was a young man, there were, practically speaking, two available paths. Either a student
gained admission to a formal university programme immediately after school, funded through
fees paid upfront, or that student's educational journey effectively ended, replaced by whatever
informal trade or labour was locally available. Information about alternative paths, when it
existed at all, traveled slowly, through word of mouth, through the rare newspaper
advertisement, through a distant relative who happened to know something useful. A young
person without access to university fees genuinely had very few visible options.
This is the world that shaped the narrow cultural story examined in Chapter Two, the deeply
held belief that university immediately after Senior High School represents the only legitimate
route to a meaningful future. That belief made complete sense for the world Robert's parents
actually grew up in. It makes considerably less sense for the world Robert is growing up in
now, and the gap between those two worlds is precisely what this chapter intends to make
visible.

"Money for education rarely arrives from one single place. It usually arrives the way a river

person forward."
ROBERT COUNTS WHAT HE HAS BUILT
Nearly a year after the accident that stopped his original plans, Robert sat down one evening
with his notebook, now considerably fuller than the thin research log it had started as, and did
something he had been avoiding for months. He added up everything he had actually built
during his season of learning and working. His savings, accumulated slowly from his technical
drawing payments, came to a modest but genuinely meaningful amount. His certificates from
Alison sat neatly in their folder. His relationship with Mr. Boateng had grown into something
resembling a genuine professional reference. For the first time since the crisis began, Robert
allowed himself to ask a question he had been avoiding, not because he feared the answer, but
because he had not yet felt ready to seriously consider it. How could he actually turn everything
he had built into real progress toward a Technical University admission or, if his savings and
other resources eventually allowed it, a delayed university programme.
This chapter exists to answer that question honestly and specifically, because vague
encouragement to seek financial support helps nobody standing in front of an actual fee
schedule. Money for education in a situation like Robert's, or like yours, rarely comes from one
single, complete source. It usually comes from several sources combined, personal savings,
formal loans, scholarships, and community or family support, each contributing a piece of a
larger, achievable total.
THE FOUR MAIN SOURCES
The first source is personal savings, exactly what Robert and Rebecca each began building in
Chapter Seven. This source is rarely large enough on its own to cover a full education, but it
92 | P a ge

===== PAGE 93 =====
matters enormously for two reasons. It demonstrates genuine commitment and discipline to
scholarship committees and loan administrators, who consistently favor applicants who have
shown they can manage money responsibly, and it covers the immediate, smaller costs,
application fees, transport, examination fees, that other funding sources rarely address.
The second source is formal government lending, specifically Ghana's Students Loan Trust
Fund, a public institution established to provide financial resources for tertiary students. As of
this writing, the fund offers loans to Ghanaian citizens who have gained admission to an
accredited tertiary institution studying an accredited programme, requiring a valid Ghana Card
and registration through the fund's official application system. The loan carries a moderate
interest rate, considerably lower than typical commercial lending, with repayment beginning
after a moratorium period following national service. It is worth noting clearly, because scams
targeting desperate applicants are unfortunately common, that the Students Loan Trust Fund
charges no fees for processing an application, and anyone requesting payment to process or
expedite a loan application should be treated as a serious warning sign.
The third source is scholarships, which exist in far greater variety than most young Ghanaians
realize. These include government administered scholarships, corporate scholarships offered
by banks, telecommunications companies, and other large employers as part of their
community investment programmes, scholarships tied to specific fields of study such as
engineering, science, or education, and smaller, less publicized scholarships offered by
churches, community organizations, and alumni associations connected to specific schools.
Scholarships vary enormously in size, some covering a small portion of fees, others covering
a student's entire programme, and the most successful applicants are typically the ones who
apply broadly to many smaller opportunities rather than waiting for a single, large scholarship
to solve their entire situation.
The fourth source is family and community sponsorship, often underestimated in its cumulative
power. This includes formal susu savings groups, common across Ghana, where a community
of savers contributes regularly to a shared fund that members can draw from for significant
expenses. It includes church congregations, many of which maintain dedicated education
support funds for members facing exactly the kind of crisis this book has been describing. It
includes extended family contributions, sometimes small individually but meaningful when
gathered from several relatives simultaneously. And increasingly, it includes small scale
crowdfunding through mobile money platforms and social media, particularly effective when

a young person's story, told honestly and specifically, reaches a wider community willing to
contribute even modest amounts.
